<p> To ensure that all students are starting at the same point, the first topic to be covered is the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units">Internation System or Units</a>, or SI. The SI specifies units to measure every conceivable quantity and a range of prefixes to help make these measurements more manageable.</p>

<p>The SI uses seven <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="'SI base unit' at Wikipedia">base units</a> in terms of which all other units can be defined (although two of these, the candela and the metre, are now defined in  terms of other base units, they are still know as such for historical reasons): 
<dl>
    <dt>metre (m)</dt>
    <dd>length</dd>
    <dt>kilogram (kg)</dt> 
    <dd>mass</dd>
    <dt>second (s)</dt>
    <dd>time</dd>
    <dt>ampere (A)</dt>
    <dd>electrical current</dd>
    <dt>kelvin (K)</dt>
    <dd>absolute temperature</dd>
    <dt>mole (mol)</dt>
    <dd>quanity of matter</dd>
    <dt>candela (cd)</dt>
    <dd>luminous intensity</dd>
</dl>
The three that we will use most frequently are the metre, the kilogram and the second.</p>

<p>In addition to the seven base units, there are numerous <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="'SI Derived Unit' at Wikipedia">derived units</a> so called because they are derived from the base units. The derived units we'll see include
<dl>
    <dt>joule (J)</dt>
    <dd>energy, work</dd>
    <dt>newton (N)</dt>
    <dd>force</dd>
</dl>
</p>

<p>In addition to the seven base units and various derived units, the SI defines a number of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_prefix" title="'SI Prefix' at Wikipedia">prefixes</a> to make it easier to deal with large and small numbers. Each prefix specifies an order of magnitude in the same way the exponent in <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_notation" title="'Scientific Notation' at Wikipedia">scientific notation</a> (which will be the topic of the next post) does. Thus one millimetre (<code>1 mm</code>) is <code>1x10<sup>-3</sup> m</code>, or </code>0.001 m</code>. The prefixes we will likely encounter in this course are:</p>
